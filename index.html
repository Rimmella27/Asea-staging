About
Asëa
ah-SAY-ah
Asëa is a personal health intelligence system for you and your family. It tracks what you eat, drink, and feel — then quietly watches for the patterns your body already knows.
Log disappears into the background. When there's something worth saying, Asëa surfaces it.
The name
The name traces to the Latin and Italian root asea — related to agio, meaning ease, comfort, and freedom from constraint. It's the feeling of a body that isn't fighting itself.
And for a bit of fun — in Quenya, Tolkien's invented language, asëa also carries the sense of ease and quiet restoration. The diaeresis over the e marks it as two syllables: ah-SAY-ah. A small detail that rewards a little attention.
Not medical advice

How Asëa notices patterns

Asëa uses a simple model: input → time → output. When you log something you consumed — a meal, a drink, a supplement — and then log a body response nearby in time, that's one data point. When the same pairing happens repeatedly, a pattern emerges.

1
You log an input — a meal, drink, or supplement added to your inventory.
2
Time passes — Asëa watches a defined window (e.g. 3 hours for caffeine, 24 hours for dairy).
3
You log an output — a symptom, glucose reading, or bowel movement.
4
This repeats — Asëa counts how many times the pairing occurred in the last 30 days.
5
A pattern surfaces — only when the count is high enough to be meaningful, not from a single event.

All processing happens entirely on your device. Nothing is sent anywhere.

Asëa only surfaces a pattern once it has happened enough times to be worth your attention. Single events are ignored entirely.

Medium
3–4 times in 30 days
A pattern worth noticing. Not yet consistent enough to be certain, but consistent enough that your body may be telling you something.
Strong
5 or more times in 30 days
A well-established pattern in your data. This pairing has shown up repeatedly and consistently within the expected time window.
New addition
Supplement or medication added recently
Based on known properties of what you added — not your logged data yet. Worth being aware of as you settle in.

Asëa is not a diagnostic tool. It finds correlations — things that tend to happen together — not causes. "Caffeine may be followed by urgency" means: in your data, these two things have occurred close together several times. It does not mean caffeine is guaranteed to cause urgency for you every time, or that it's the only factor involved.

This framing is intentional. Your body is complex, your life has many variables, and Asëa has only a partial view. The goal is to give you something worth noticing — not a verdict.

If a pattern surprises you or concerns you, it's always worth discussing with your doctor. Asëa's data can be a useful conversation starter.

Research in behavioral psychology consistently shows that people change habits most effectively when they discover connections themselves — not when they're told what to do.

Asëa is designed around this. When you see "caffeine may be followed by urgency — 4 out of 5 times," something clicks that a general warning never achieves. That recognition is yours. The next time you reach for coffee, you'll notice differently.

That moment of noticing — what psychologists call an implementation intention — is where real change begins. Asëa doesn't tell you what to do. It shows you what you've already been experiencing, clearly enough that you can decide what, if anything, to do about it.

About this list

The foods in Asëa's list were selected using four evidence-based lenses:

Functional Medicine
Treats food as information — not just fuel. Focuses on gut microbiome health, inflammation as a root driver of chronic symptoms, and the whole-system effect of what you eat.
Clinical Nutrition
Glycemic load, micronutrient density, fiber types, and macronutrient timing. The science behind what registered dietitians recommend.
Integrative & Naturopathic
Phytonutrients, polyphenols, and synergistic food combinations. Example: turmeric paired with black pepper increases absorption dramatically. These pairings are built into the benefit data.
Behavioral Nutrition
Why certain foods reduce the urge to reach for something harmful. Satiety signaling, craving architecture, and habit formation — because knowing what to eat is only half the equation.

Each food shows small tags summarizing its primary health contributions. These are not medical claims — they reflect the research-supported properties of each food when eaten as part of a balanced diet.

These same tags connect to Asëa's insight engine. When you log a food consistently, the engine can detect patterns between what you eat and how your body responds.

Use the quick-add field to add any food not in the list. Custom items won't have benefit tags — that's expected. They'll still appear on your shopping list and carry over to your Store tab when marked as needed.

About Bowel Tracking

The Bristol Stool Scale is a simple tool used by doctors to describe stool consistency on a 1–7 range. Asëa uses a simplified 5-category version to make logging fast and pattern detection meaningful.

1–2
Hard
Separate hard lumps or a lumpy sausage shape. Often linked to slow transit or low fiber and fluid intake.
3
Firm
Sausage-shaped with surface cracks. On the drier side but within a normal range for many people.
4
Normal ✓
Smooth, soft, and easy to pass. The benchmark for healthy digestion.
5
Loose
Soft blobs with defined edges. Can be normal or a sign of faster transit time.
6–7
Watery
Mushy or fully liquid. Faster transit than usual — hydration and food choices often play a role.
Asëa tracks patterns — it doesn't interpret them. If you notice an unusual color consistently, it's usually advisable to speak with your doctor.
Brown
The typical healthy range. Variations in shade are normal day to day.
Green
Often related to diet — leafy greens, food dye — or faster transit time.
Yellow
Can be linked to diet or faster digestion. Worth noting if it persists.
Black / Tarry
May be related to iron supplements or certain foods. If it persists and isn't diet-related, it's usually advisable to speak with your doctor.
Bright Red
Can be caused by food (beets, red dye) or other factors. If you notice this consistently without an obvious dietary cause, it's usually advisable to speak with your doctor.
Pale / Clay
An unusual color worth mentioning to your doctor if it occurs more than occasionally.

HOW WE COUNT YOUR HYDRATION

Not all drinks are created equal. Coffee and alcohol prompt your body to lose water faster than plain water does. The Drink Ring tracks total fluid intake, while the Effective (Net) Hydration bar shows the actual amount your body keeps.

THE SCIENCE

The "8 glasses a day" rule isn't based on modern research. According to NASEM, hydration needs vary by person. A simple starting point: Your Weight (lbs) ÷ 2 = Target Ounces per day.

About Blood Pressure
What do the two numbers mean?

Blood pressure is measured as two numbers — written as SYS/DIA, for example 120/80.

SYS
Systolic — the top number
The pressure in your arteries when your heart beats and pushes blood out. This is the higher of the two numbers.
DIA
Diastolic — the bottom number
The pressure in your arteries when your heart is resting between beats. This is the lower of the two numbers.

Both numbers matter. A reading of 120/80 mmHg is widely considered a healthy baseline for most adults, though what's normal varies by person.

Asëa tracks your readings over time. It doesn't interpret whether any individual reading is healthy — that's a conversation for your doctor.
How does blood pressure work?

Your heart pumps blood continuously through a network of blood vessels. Blood pressure is simply the force of that blood pushing against the vessel walls.

When the vessels are relaxed and open, blood flows easily and pressure stays lower. When they tighten — due to stress, certain foods, or other factors — the heart has to push harder, and pressure rises.

Blood pressure naturally fluctuates throughout the day. It tends to be lower when you're resting and higher during activity or stress. This is normal. Patterns over time — not single readings — are what matter most.

What can affect blood pressure?

Many everyday factors are associated with blood pressure changes. Asëa can help you notice whether any of these seem to coincide with your own readings.

🧂
Salt & sodium
High sodium intake is one of the most commonly noted dietary factors associated with elevated blood pressure in some people.
☕
Caffeine
Caffeine can cause a short-term rise in blood pressure. The effect varies considerably between individuals.
🍷
Alcohol
Regular or heavy alcohol consumption is associated with higher blood pressure over time.
💧
Hydration
Dehydration can cause blood pressure to drop or fluctuate. Staying well hydrated supports stable circulation.
😮‍💨
Stress
Stress triggers hormones that temporarily raise blood pressure. Chronic stress may contribute to sustained elevation.
These are observed associations — not causes. Everyone responds differently. Asëa helps you notice your own patterns rather than applying general rules.
About Glucose
What does mg/dL mean?

mg/dL stands for milligrams per decilitre — it's simply a measure of how much glucose (sugar) is present in a small volume of blood.

A higher number means more glucose in the bloodstream at that moment. The number on its own doesn't tell you much — context matters enormously: when you last ate, how active you've been, and your personal baseline all affect what a reading means for you.

Asëa records your readings and tracks patterns over time. It does not interpret whether any reading is in a healthy range — that's a conversation for your doctor or healthcare provider.
Why does context matter?

The same glucose number means very different things depending on when you measured it. This is why Asëa asks you to log the context with every reading.

🌙
Fasting
Measured after at least 8 hours without eating. Often done first thing in the morning. Considered the most stable baseline reading.
🍽️
Before Meal
Taken just before eating. Helps establish a pre-meal baseline to compare against after-meal readings.
⏱️
After Meal
Usually measured 1–2 hours after eating. Shows how your body responds to the food you just consumed.
🛏️
Bed Time
Taken before sleep. Useful for understanding overnight patterns and whether evening meals affect morning levels.
What can affect glucose levels?

Many everyday factors are associated with glucose fluctuations. Asëa can help you notice whether any of these coincide with changes in your own readings.

🍬
Sugar & refined carbs
Foods that digest quickly — white bread, sugary drinks, sweets — are commonly associated with rapid glucose rises.
🕐
Meal timing
Eating late at night or skipping meals can affect glucose patterns, particularly overnight and fasting readings.
💧
Hydration
Dehydration concentrates the blood, which can cause glucose readings to appear higher than when well hydrated.
😮‍💨
Stress
Stress hormones can raise blood glucose even without eating. Some people notice elevated readings during high-stress periods.
🏃
Activity
Physical activity generally helps the body use glucose more efficiently. The timing and intensity of exercise both play a role.
These are observed associations — not rules. Your body is individual. Asëa helps you notice your own patterns rather than applying general assumptions.
